The Valencia monitoring networkRVVCCA · 12 stations · January 2021 – December 2022 · Hourly

Valencia's RVVCCA network maintains twelve active monitoring stations across the metropolitan area. The 2021–2022 hourly dataset — downloaded directly from Open Data Valencia — provides the foundational baseline for STRATA's divergence analysis. Stations span traffic-exposed urban canyons, residential periphery, coastal and port positions, and dedicated meteorological posts. Each records hourly PM2.5, PM10, NO₂, and O₃ alongside meteorological parameters.

This is STRATA's reference dataset: two full years of hourly observations capturing diurnal cycles, calima episodes, seasonal transitions, and long-term atmospheric patterns across Valencia's monitoring network.

The representation gapCustom multi-pollutant composite index vs PM10-only public index

Many public-facing AQI representations simplify atmospheric conditions into a single dominant pollutant proxy, often emphasising PM10. This carries a structural cost: any episode where gas-phase pollutants elevate multi-pollutant burden while particulate matter stays moderate will be systematically underrepresented.

Strata quantifies this as the CMPI divergence: the absolute difference between a custom multi-pollutant composite index — weighting normalised PM2.5 (40%), PM10 (30%), NO₂ (20%), and O₃ (10%) on a 0–100 scale — and the public PM10-only index on the same scale. The CMPI is not a regulatory index; it is an exploratory comparative framework.

When the gap peaks — nocturnal NO₂ accumulationReal RVVCCA 2021–2022 · Peak — · Min —

The CMPI divergence follows a pronounced diurnal cycle. The gap peaks at — (mean — index points) and reaches its minimum at — (— points) — driven almost entirely by NO₂.

During the night, the planetary boundary layer collapses under radiative cooling, trapping combustion-sourced NO₂ at ground level. The public PM10-only index is entirely blind to this accumulation — PM10 also rises overnight, but the CMPI gives NO₂ an explicit 20% weight, opening a large representational gap. Public AQI is least representative precisely during early-morning hours when many people check conditions before commuting.

By afternoon, photochemical activity converts NO₂ to secondary products, mixing height increases, and ventilation disperses both PM10 and NO₂. The two indices partially converge. This pattern is consistent with nocturnal boundary layer dynamics documented for Mediterranean urban environments (Millán et al., 2000).

Atmospheric complexityShannon entropy · Source attribution · Mean ACI: —

The Atmospheric Complexity Index (ACI) is a custom entropy-based metric applied to four rule-based source attribution scores (Saharan, urban, marine, stagnation) estimated from PM ratios and wind speed. ACI = 0 indicates single-source dominance; ACI = 1 indicates maximum source ambiguity. Methodological note: the ACI is a heuristic research construct, not calibrated against receptor modelling or back-trajectory analysis.

Seasonal structure — episode-driven divergenceSeasonal CMPI divergence · Calima vs DANA comparison · 2024

At daily resolution, the divergence structure reveals itself through seasonal patterns and discrete atmospheric episodes. The nocturnal NO₂ accumulation cycle identified in the 2021–2022 hourly data averages out at daily resolution — what remains are the episode-level signals that persist across full days.

The calima signal remains detectable: days where PM10 spikes above the 3-day rolling baseline with a high dust ratio (PM10/PM2.5 > 3.0) show elevated divergence consistent with the baseline findings. The DANA period (October 29 – mid-December 2024) introduces a structurally different signal: flood-derived resuspension of terrestrial sediment produced coarse aerosol with a local wind signature rather than the southerly Saharan transport pattern.

This comparison — same analytical framework applied to two physically distinct dust event types — is the primary scientific contribution of the 2024 dataset extension.

05

Calima vs DANA — two dust types, one frameworkSaharan mineral dust vs flood-derived terrestrial resuspension · 2024

The October 29, 2024 DANA flood created an atmospheric anomaly in Valencia's monitoring record: flood-derived resuspension of terrestrial sediment — road dust, river sediment, agricultural soil — introduced coarse particulate matter with a fundamentally different origin than Saharan mineral transport. Both events drive elevated PM10 and PM10/PM2.5 ratios. Both therefore affect the same representational gap in the same direction. But their atmospheric origins, chemical compositions, and health-relevant characteristics differ.

Strata detects DANA events using the same dual criterion as calima (PM10 spike + high dust ratio), restricted to the October 29 – December 15 2024 window, with a slightly relaxed ratio threshold (2.5 vs 3.0) reflecting the flood sediment's potentially finer composition. Calima takes precedence when both criteria fire simultaneously.

Finding 03 · DANA as a novel representational failure mode

The DANA period offers the first systematic look at representational divergence during a genuine public health emergency in Valencia. The public PM10-only index detects elevated particulate matter during both calima and DANA — but cannot distinguish Saharan mineral aerosol from flood-derived terrestrial sediment, two types with different compositional profiles in the epidemiological literature. This is the same structural limitation, applied to a new atmospheric event type.
